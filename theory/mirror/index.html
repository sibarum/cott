1. Motivation

Many theories of calculus exist. Various notations have been popularized, each finding its own niche: such as Lagrange for brevity or Leibniz for specificity. The use of the letter d indicates a total derivative while the ∂ indicates a partial derivative. The formulas change depending on the context, ranging from linear maps to multiplicative and even logarithmic maps, which also differ depending on the number of variables and which relationships are being measured. There is, indeed, a seemingly endless space of possible calculi, all sharing common characteristics and goals while appearing at the surface to be entirely unrelated.

I don't wish to diminish the contributions of mathematicians throughout history who made modern mathematics possible. Their names belong in our books, to be taught to students, and remembered for generations to come. But we do a disservice to ourselves and to humanity as a whole if we don't address problems with a clear head and down-to-earth common sense.

Math is harder to learn than it needs to be. Math has a notation problem, and it's precisely the result of inheriting all the historical tradition without embracing the art of theoretic reframing.

1.1 Theory Consolidation

One type of theoretic reframing is theory consolidation. This involves merging several, disjointed theories into a single, cohesive theory. The resulting theory may end up quite different from the source material, and ideally simpler than any original theory itself. The goal is unification: getting more utility with less mental investment is criminally underrated in the mathematical zeitgeist of modern academia.

Using fewer symbols to accomplish the same thing makes theories easier to learn, analyze, and extend. Undeniably, a pupil and practitioner alike will benefit from formulating a unifying theory such that all its symmetries and expressive capability is easily generalized. And yet this practice is rarely exercised, and is often perceived as controversial or somehow disrespectful.

The deal-breaker is that theoretic reframing often requires making foundational changes across other, well-established theories. This is seen as an insurmountable obstacle - almost as if the author of the reframing would need to re-prove every theorem that has ever been written which derives from the impacted theories - as if the ontology of the universe itself shifts with the penmanship of a theorist.

In reality, a healthy reframing of a class of theories can illuminate the true nature of our universe in a way that enables the future generations to more easily learn, participate in, and appreciate the art and science of math. And isn't that the greatest good we can accomplish with our talents? It's it our duty?

2. A Different Kind of Zero

Mirror Calculus builds upon Traction Theory, an algebra with a non-absorbing zero. Similar to a ring and field, a traction is a general-purpose algebraic object with restrictions around zero notably absent from the definitions.

The classic assumption 0*x = 0 does not hold.

0*x != 0

Instead, we conserve information, only erasing information under universal invariance, the operational identities, and keeping everything else as residual terms.

x-x=null

But the residuals are not merely formal bookkeeping terms. We find by analyzing the Chebyshev Polynomials, real useful structure emerges from arithmetic of the totalized zero. More on this later.

2.1 Emergence from Void

It's ironic how much structure can exist within a symbol that represents nothingness, but we must recognise this apparent irony as mere ignorance. If all known existence can emerge from primeval void, why do we assume void a dead end? Granted we cannot witness the void directly, and all we know with certainty is it gave birth to everything we can witness, why do we assume the transition from void to existence a rare or unusual occurrence?

Let's start by changing our assumptions. Zero is no longer emptiness, no longer an exception, no longer a dead end. Instead, let's assume zero is the beginning, a place from which endless structure may emerge. What kind of structure would we expect to emerge from zero? For this we consider the only example we know of: our own universe. Assuming it emerged from nothingness, what does this tell us about the nature and quality of things that emerge from voids?

We consider the lowest foundations of physics. Thermodynamics imply a balance of structure, symmetry of dynamics, and consistency of causality. Quantum physics implies chaotic emergence, statistical averaging, and temporal, quantized distinctions that always resolve, eventually. These concepts are not at odds, in fact they tell us a great deal about the nature of the void.

In quantum mechanics, we observe virtual particles spontaneously phasing into and out of existence, sometimes long enough to be observed, but always balanced out by a corresponding counter-particle or packet of energy - as thermodynamics would mandate.

And in classic algebra, one might solve an equation by drawing structure from the void. By adding a value alongside its inverse, conservation isn't violated, and consistency is preserved with exact precision.

Creating algebraic structure from nothing is, therefore, permissible so long as the total offset of the addition is zero. Or, at the least, contextually invariant. This ensures consistency without diminishing potential utility.

The complex algebra of traction zero is one interpretation of this concept. Complex arithmetic, when described as imaginary rotation with magnitude zero, can indeed emerge from the mere permission of its permanence. Mirror Calculus, then, steps into a bolder frame of reference: How far can zero be pushed, and what are its limits?

3. The Polymorphic Zero

Zero is a reservoir of latent structure. Within zero, all possible structures exist in perfect balance, ready to be summoned anywhere for any reason, as long as any nonzero emergence is accompanied by its inverse or "mirrored" counterpart. This is a well-known fact, and commonplace in ordinary mathematics.

Consider the following illustration of arbitrary structure emerging from nothingness:

0=0
0=0+a-a
0-0+a=a
a=a
a*a^{-1}=a*a^{-1}
{a*a^{-1}}/a^{-1}={a*a^{-1}}*a
a=a^2*a^{-1}
{1/a^{-1}} + a/a^{-1}={1/a^{-1}} + a^2
{a+1}/a^{-1}={1/a^{-1}} + a^2*{a^{-1}/a^{-1}}
{a+1}/a^{-1}=(1/a^{-1} + a/a^{-1})*{a/a}
{a+1}/a^{-1}=a + a^2
a={a + a^2}/{a+1}

It's easy to see how this exercise could go on forever, leading to an infinite landscape of equivalences, many of which are not trivially trivial.

3.1 Differentials

In classic calculus, the differential is a confused concept. It is seen more as an incomplete derivative, or as a kind of variable, rather than a function of infinitesimals. Then differentials appear in other areas of mathematics, but by an entirely different name: the jet operation and Taylor polynomials. This is an academic failure of cohesion: nobody benefits from these topics being disjointed.

In mirror calculus, a mirror differential is any functor applied to an expression with the following characteristics:

  1. The functor evaluates to an expression with a zero magnitude in the dimension of measurement.
  2. The functor conserves all information of the target expression.

Information conservation is standard operating procedure under Constructive Operational Type Theory. Differentials frequently serve as a diagnostic, so they extract or add information to an expression, as long as this information is maintained via residuals (hence "zero magnitude in the dimension of measurement").

Consider the following Taylor differential over some function f:

0*f(x+0)

Notice:

  • The expression is multiplied by traction zero, ensuring a zero magnitude as per rule 1.
  • We are not subtracting the identity, as this would erase the constant y-offset and violate rule 2.

This, and any other mirror differential, may be used as a substitution anytime an expression comes within multiplicative association with zero. For example:

f(x) = 0*(x^{2}+3*x-4)
0*f(x+0) = 0*((x+0)^{2}+3*(x+0)-4)
0*f(x+0) = 0*((x^2+2*0*x+0^2)+(3*x+3*0)-4)
0*f(x+0) = 0*(x^2+3*x-4+2*0*x+3*0+0^2)
0*f(x+0) = (0*x^2+3*0*x-4*0)+(2*0^2*x+3*0^2)+0^3

We now have the complete Taylor differential, from which we may extract terms.

w*(0*f(x+0)) = f(x+0)
f(x+0) = x^2+3*x-4+(2*0*x+3*0)+0^2

And we use the projection operator to truncate zero-terms. This recovers the original function exactly.

P(f(x+0)) = x^2+3*x-4

And to recover the first derivative:

w*f(x+0) = w*x^2+w*3*x-w*4+(2*x+3)+0
P(w*f(x+0)) = 2*x+3
